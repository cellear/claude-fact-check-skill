# Example Fact-Check: Coffee and Cancer Risk

## Original Post

> "BREAKING: New study shows coffee consumption reduces cancer risk by 80%. Researchers at major university confirm what coffee lovers have known all along! #CoffeeSavesLives #HealthNews"

## Fact-Check Report

### Main Claim Identified

**Primary Claim**: Coffee consumption reduces cancer risk by 80% according to a new study.

*If you think a different claim is more important, let me know and I'll analyze that instead.*

---

### Triage

**Status**: Disputed - Contains significant exaggerations and misleading framing

The claim references health research with a specific statistic (80%), which requires verification. The vague reference to "researchers at major university" and the sensationalist tone raise immediate red flags.

---

### Evidence Summary

**Supporting Evidence:**
- **Original Study** (Academic research, published 2024): A meta-analysis in a peer-reviewed journal found modest associations between coffee consumption and reduced risk of specific cancers (liver, endometrial). The study reported relative risk reductions ranging from 12-20% for specific cancer types, NOT 80% overall cancer risk reduction.
  - Authors: Named researchers from credible institutions
  - Nuanced findings with careful language about correlation vs. causation
  
- **Health News Blog** (Individual blogger, published 2024): Reported "80% reduction" by cherry-picking a single data point from a subgroup analysis in the original study (post-menopausal women with specific genetic markers and liver cancer only).
  - Blog post was sensationalized for clicks
  - Author has no medical credentials
  
**Disputing Evidence:**
- **Multiple Cancer Research Organizations**: Emphasize that while some studies show modest associations, coffee does not "reduce cancer risk by 80%"
  - American Cancer Society notes evidence is mixed and effect sizes are small
  - No credible source supports an 80% overall cancer risk reduction claim

---

### Source Independence Analysis ⚠️

**Citation Chain:**
```
Original peer-reviewed study (2024)
  ↓
Health News Blog (misrepresents study)
  ↓
Viral social media posts (cite blog, not original study)
```

**Independence Assessment:**

⚠️ **The viral claim does NOT have independent verification.** All supporting sources trace back to a single study, and the "80% reduction" claim appears to originate from a single blog post that misrepresented a narrow subgroup finding from that study.

The original study:
- Found 12-20% risk reductions for specific cancer types
- Did NOT claim 80% overall cancer risk reduction
- Included careful caveats about limitations

**Credibility Factors:**
- **Original Study**: High credibility - peer-reviewed, named authors, institutional backing
- **Blog Post**: Low credibility - individual author, no editorial oversight, history of sensationalist headlines
- **Viral Post**: No credibility - anonymous, no source citation, clickbait framing

---

### Ideological Alignment

Not applicable to this claim - health research crosses ideological lines. However, there's a commercial angle: coffee industry and "wellness influencers" may have incentive to amplify positive coffee news.

---

### Rhetorical Issues 🚨

**Headline-Text Mismatch:**
- Claims "new study" shows 80% reduction, but no such study exists with that finding

**Cherry-Picking:**
- The blog post cherry-picked a single subgroup finding (post-menopausal women with specific markers, liver cancer only) and misrepresented it as overall cancer risk reduction

**Appeal to Authority:**
- "Researchers at major university" - deliberately vague, no names or institutions cited
- Prevents verification and inflates credibility

**Sensationalism:**
- "BREAKING" and "what coffee lovers have known all along" create emotional engagement rather than informing
- Hashtags like #CoffeeSavesLives are hyperbolic

**Misrepresentation of Science:**
- Original study showed correlation, not causation
- Effect sizes were modest (12-20%), not dramatic (80%)
- Applied to specific cancer types, not overall cancer risk

---

### Bottom Line

**Verdict**: FALSE - The claim is not supported by evidence.

**Confidence Level**: High

**Key Facts:**
- No credible study shows coffee reduces overall cancer risk by 80%
- Original research found modest (12-20%) associations for specific cancer types
- The "80%" figure appears to come from misrepresentation of a narrow subgroup finding
- This is a clear case of science misrepresentation for viral engagement

**Recommendation**: Do not share this claim. If discussing coffee and health, cite the actual research with appropriate nuance: some studies suggest modest associations between coffee consumption and reduced risk of certain specific cancers, but evidence is not conclusive and effect sizes are small.

---

## Methodology Notes

This fact-check demonstrates several key principles:

1. **Source Independence is Critical**: Multiple sources citing the same study ≠ independent verification
2. **Check the Original Source**: The viral claim dramatically misrepresented the actual research
3. **Watch for Vague Authority Appeals**: "Major university" and "researchers" without names = red flag
4. **Cherry-Picking Detection**: Finding the "80%" required looking at subgroup analyses
5. **Headline-Text Disconnect**: The sensational claim doesn't match the actual evidence

This is a textbook example of how legitimate research gets distorted into viral misinformation through selective reporting and sensationalism.
